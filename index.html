Graphlets analysis of human brain's structural and functional connectomes

The human brain can be modeled as a graph, a collection of nodes connected by links following a certain pattern. Following my MSc thesis research work on network neuroscience, I found a new interesting approach for studying graphs. Graphlets are small, connected, induced subgraphs. Different nodes in a graphlet can have different topological properties and they are called orbits. The distribution of orbits and their correlation can reveal interesting topological features of a Graph. I computed the orbit distributions for structural (DWI) and functional (MEG and fMRI) connectomes. Orbits can be roughly divided into two groups: central, if the node is found at the centre of the graphlet, or peripheral if elsewhere. A node in a Graph can appear several times as central, peripheral orbits or both. To better understand the role and usefulness of this measure, I computed the partial correlation between the central and peripheral orbits count and some classical graph theory measures, like the degree, clustering coefficient or centrality measures. It appears that highly connected nodes or hubs in the brain can have a high central or peripheral orbit count. It is the case for provincial hubs and connector hubs respectively. Indeed, peripheral hubs correlated positively with participation coefficient (especially for structural connectomes) while central hubs correlated with the within-module z-score. The graphlet approach didn't bring any novelty for my project (though it offered an efficient way to summarise the topological property of the connectomes). I then decided to move to more deep learning-focused projects. This project will be included in my PhD thesis as chapter 1 and submitted in a network neuroscience journal. Deep Learning for Parameter Recovery from a Neural Mass Model of Perceptual Decision Making

In Psychology, parameter recovery refers to the problem of finding the best parameters of a model for fitting the experimental data. The developing of more biologically plausible computational models of cognition has offered a significant improvement in the predictive power at the cost of a higher complexity posing increasing challenges on parameter recovery. Here, we present a deep learning approach to recover parameters of a two-variables neural mass model simulating evidence accumulation during perceptual decision-making. We show that our algorithm is able to recovery well specific set of parameters but might fail when trying to predict combinations of parameters with a high degree of interaction, i.e. parameters that have inherently similar effects on the model’s output. Thus, our study suggests that deep learning for parameter recovery should go together with a carefully designed experiment to study the effects of different parameters that are not richly interacting. CNN and RNN Ensemble for Random Dot Motion Discrimination Task (ongoing)

Following my work on parameter recovery of the Wang-Wong model for the random dot motion task, I was curious to see if I could observe evidence accumulation in Deep Learning algorithms. I started with an input modeled as a gaussian noise with average equal to the motion coherence of the dots. This is the kind of input the LIP region in the human brain should use to accumulate evidence and "take" a decision. A single unit RNN was sufficient to solve the left/right discrimination task with similar accuracy and reaction times or RT as humans. I obtained the RT distribution by setting a threshold on the cumulative sum of the RNN hidden state and assumed the decision is made when the threshold is reached. However, I later noticed that the RNN was not needed as similar results can be obtained by a simple cumulative sum (evidence accumulation) directly on the input. I then generated a video of the random dot motion discrimination task using PsychoPy, and passed each frame to a 1 filter CNN. The outputs were concatenated into a sequence and passed to a 10 hidden units RNN. Once trained, this simple ensemble is able to solve the task with performance similar to humans. I'm currently experimenting on the CNN output, analizyng the CNN filter and trying to reduce the output (e.g., with pooling) to see if the output sequence is similar to a random gaussian noise with non-zero mean. After that, I plan to study the RNN weights and outputs to see if it performs some sort of evidence accumulation as the human brain does. Neural Decoding of a Monkey's Arm Movements on Computed Neurons' Connectivity using Graph Neural Networks (ongoing)

Effectively translating neural activity into behavioral output is an open problem in neuroscience with potential application in BCI and neuroprosthetics. During Neuromatch Academy - Deep Learning, me and my team have been working on decoding the neural activity of a mouse turning a wheel left or right based on a visual contrast task. We tried a mix of techniques with RNNs having the best cost-effectiveness ratio. Inspired by the positive results, I decide to apply a similar framework on a 2D motor task performed by two Monkeys. The first monkey performed the task three times, with about 50 neurons from the premotor cortex (PMd). The second monkey performed a single task with both motor (M1) and premotor areas (PMd) while recording 67 and 94 neurons respectively. The dataset includes position, speed, acceleration and force of the arm as behavioral output. I preprocessed the data using Prof. Kording Lab's toolkit for neural decoding and reimplemented the model with the best cost-effectiveness ration (again, RNN) using PyTorch. The results show the best decoding performance for speed. Interestingly, higher number of neurons didn't bring higher decoding performance, probably because it has been observed that motor and premotor areas are not only involved in direct motor control, but also backward task like motor learning. I'm currently trying to use Graph Neural Network (GNN) on inferred connectivity among neurons using cross-correlation to see if I can improve the decoding performance and/or efficiency.

Comparison of humans, model-free and model-based Reinforcement Learning Agents at Playing Pac-Man (designing/looking for collaborations)

During my PhD I haven't had the chance to study Reinforcement Learning. Now, my lab mate is about to start collecting behavioral data together with fMRI scanning of people playing Pac-Man. I couldn't resist the urge to get involved so I started designing a project using these data. I'm particularly interested in comparing human strategies with Reinforcement Learning agents to learn more about planning. Strategies have been studied on non human primate and a model of weighted strategies with a softmax policy to select the action was able to reproduce the monkey behaviour with high accuracy. It would be interesting to study the difference between humans, model-free and model-based RL agents in term of strategies and planning and see if I could use this knowledge to improve the performance of RL agents over the state-of-the-art. Thanks to the very kind availability of two Senior Research Scientists from DeepMind, I have collected precious feedback and created a detailed research plan. I'm currently looking for a Research Assistant/Internship position for a couple of months to develop this project and submit it to a top-tier AI conference (ICLR, NeurIPS, ICML).
